Speaking of Mesmerism in the twenty-first century, we find ourselves before a great unknown. Although there was a time when this concept was recurrent, in the present times of the abyss, where the binary code we visualize is uncertain and, in most cases, no longer human, society is not familiar with these concepts.

In ancient times, alchemy and Hermeticism, among others, favored the creation of cultural spaces, normally initiatory, where the instinctive connection with occultism and sensory capacities was established in order to help man understand the origin of the world.

By contrast, the current paradigm is extremely different. As in a dystopian book, the tools we have created to make our work easier and allow us a more ephemeral life, full of pleasures and with a reduced amount of work, have begun to replace the human being, becoming the absolute protagonists of the near future. I am not going to waste the reader’s time by giving a masterclass on what Mesmerism is or on the initial concepts that describe the phenomenon. Nor am I going to provide abstract descriptions of animal magnetism or of its creator; rather, with the permission of those who read my words, and if G-d allows it, I shall begin directly from the end.

The principles of the universe are clearly scientific; however, do they correspond to our science? Do they correspond to human laws? Can we define the universe as an environment that functions according to the laws of our species, or at least according to our discoveries? Certainly, mathematical language is the most accurate, and the one that contributes to every step we take; every force, every physical particle, everything responds to science and, specifically, to mathematical principles.

But science, if it is truly science, must not become a prison built by its own instruments. To measure is not always to understand. To classify is not always to dominate. Man has often believed that reality ends where his method ends, and this is one of the great errors of our age. The universe does not ask permission from human language in order to exist, nor does the invisible cease to operate because the modern mind has not yet given it an acceptable name.

This is the first door through which Mesmerism must be understood.

If Mesmerism is reduced only to the old doctrine of animal magnetism, understood as a physical fluid moving from one body to another, then caution is necessary. That specific explanation remains uncertain. Perhaps it was incomplete. Perhaps it was symbolically true but materially imprecise. Perhaps Mesmer, like many men who touch a real force before their century is ready to name it, described with the vocabulary of his time something that belonged to a deeper order of human influence.

But if by Mesmerism we mean the capacity to act upon the human being through attention, suggestion, symbolic authority, trance, rhythm, silence, presence and directed imagination, then Mesmerism is not false. It exists. It has always existed. It exists in the hypnotic act, in the ritual act, in the magnetic authority of certain individuals, in the power of the gaze, in the command of the voice, in the atmosphere created by a room, a symbol, a gesture, a word, a silence.

This is the point where the modern world becomes blind. It rejects the old word, but it continues to obey the old force.

The hypnotic level of Mesmerism is real. Not as fantasy, not as theatre, not as the vulgar spectacle of a stage performer, but as a functional art of consciousness. The human being can be guided into altered states of attention. His perception can be narrowed. His resistance can be softened. His imagination can be made active. His body can respond to an inner command. His pain, fear, memory, desire and sense of identity can be modified by suggestion when the correct conditions are created.

This is Mesmerism.

It does not matter whether the eighteenth century called it animal magnetism and the twenty-first century calls it hypnosis, suggestion, trance, psychosomatic response or altered consciousness. Names change. The force remains. The old temple becomes the clinic. The ritual chamber becomes the therapeutic room. The initiatic silence becomes guided induction. The operator changes his clothes, but the structure is ancient.

There is always one who directs and one who receives. There is always a threshold. There is always a moment in which ordinary consciousness loses its throne and another language begins to operate.

This language is not the language of ordinary reason. It is older. It belongs to image, rhythm, expectation and surrender. It speaks to the body before it speaks to the intellect. It does not argue; it impresses. It does not explain; it enters. It does not convince through logic, but through symbolic command. This is why Mesmerism cannot be understood only by those who demand mechanical explanations. The Mesmeric act happens in the borderland between mind and flesh, between imagination and nervous system, between belief and physiological response.

The rationalist will say: this is only suggestion.

But suggestion is not “only” anything. Suggestion is one of the sovereign powers of human existence. A man can be destroyed by a word. A soldier can be sent to death by an image of honour. A patient can suffer or improve according to expectation. A crowd can become obedient through rhythm and voice. A child can carry for life a sentence pronounced by his father. A lover can become enslaved by a gaze. A people can be moved by a myth. Civilization itself is built upon repeated suggestions that become law, identity and destiny.

If this is suggestion, then suggestion is empire.

Mesmerism is the conscious use of that empire.

The Mesmeric operator, when he is serious, does not merely perform tricks. He constructs a field. He creates the conditions in which the subject becomes receptive to transformation. The room, the voice, the pace, the silence, the confidence, the gaze, the repetition, the expectation: all these elements form the architecture of the hypnotic world. Within that architecture, the ordinary self begins to loosen. The person becomes more permeable. The imagination becomes more vivid. The body begins to listen.

This is why Hermetic Mesmerism and hypnotic Mesmerism are not separate in essence. They are two aspects of the same art. Hermeticism provides the symbolic universe; hypnosis provides the operative method. Hermeticism gives meaning, hierarchy, correspondence and sacred atmosphere. Hypnosis gives access, direction and technical efficiency. Together, they form a disciplined art of influence over consciousness.

Animal Mesmerism, in the strict material sense, may remain uncertain. But Hermetic and hypnotic Mesmerism are real because they act. They produce effects. They transform the internal state of the human being. They do not need to be defended as superstition, because their existence is visible wherever attention is captured and the mind is led beyond its ordinary frontier.

The error of the modern world is that it believes Mesmerism disappeared. It did not disappear. It changed masks.

Advertising is degraded Mesmerism. Political propaganda is collective Mesmerism. Cinema is visual Mesmerism. Social media is algorithmic Mesmerism. The endless screen is a hypnotic mirror. The modern subject, who laughs at old initiates and magnetizers, spends hours every day in a state of induced attention before luminous symbols designed to shape his desire, his fear, his memory and his behaviour.

Modernity has not destroyed Mesmerism. It has industrialized it.

The difference is that the ancient operator, at his highest level, sought transformation, initiation, healing, awakening or inner command. The modern operator seeks consumption, obedience, addiction and distraction. One acted through silence and symbol; the other acts through noise and repetition. One understood the sacred danger of influence; the other sells the soul of man by the second.

For this reason, to recover Mesmerism is not to return naïvely to the past. It is to reclaim an art that has been stolen, vulgarized and mechanized. It is to understand that man is not free merely because he possesses information. He is free only when he understands the forces that act upon his attention. And Mesmerism is, above all, the science and art of attention directed toward transformation.

The Mesmeric Arts therefore must be defended.

They must be defended against the charlatan, who reduces them to fraud. They must be defended against the materialist, who reduces them to nothing. They must be defended against the entertainer, who reduces them to spectacle. They must be defended against the algorithm, which uses their principles without honour, without initiation and without responsibility.

The true Mesmerist is not a clown, nor a magician in the vulgar sense. He is an operator of consciousness. He understands that the human being is porous, symbolic and suggestible. He understands that the body is not separated from imagination. He understands that the word can become sensation, that the image can become command, that the ritual can become transformation, that the gaze can become law inside the inner theatre of the subject.

This does not mean that all things are possible. It does not mean that fantasy should replace discipline. It does not mean that every occult claim must be accepted. On the contrary, true Mesmerism requires severity. It requires ethics, control, knowledge of the psyche, knowledge of symbol, knowledge of silence. The one who cannot govern himself has no right to influence another. The one who seeks only domination has already corrupted the art.

But the existence of corruption does not negate the existence of the art. False priests do not disprove religion. Bad physicians do not disprove medicine. Fraudulent magnetizers do not disprove the hypnotic reality of Mesmerism.

The conclusion, therefore, must be clear.

The old animal fluid remains uncertain. It may be metaphor, error, intuition or lost language. But Mesmerism itself, understood at the Hermetic and hypnotic level, exists. It is functional. It is effective. It acts through attention, symbol, suggestion, trance, expectation and the directed imagination. It is one of the oldest arts of mankind, older than modern psychology, older than clinical vocabulary, older than the machines that now imitate its power.

Mesmerism is not dead.

It survives in the hypnotic chamber, in the ritual space, in the voice that commands the nervous system, in the symbol that reorganizes the soul, in the silence that prepares surrender, in the gaze that concentrates the will, in the word that enters the body like an invisible hand.

And perhaps this is why it frightens the modern world.

Because Mesmerism reveals that man is not the sovereign rational animal he pretends to be. He is also dream, body, fear, image, obedience, memory and desire. He is open. He is influenceable. He is capable of being wounded by symbols and healed by symbols. He is capable of entering another state and returning changed.

The animal magnetism of Mesmer may remain veiled in uncertainty, but the hypnotic truth of Mesmerism stands before us with undeniable force: consciousness can be directed, the body can respond, and the invisible architecture of meaning can transform the visible life of man.

That is not superstition.

That is Mesmeric Art.

**Nobody will ever give you the solution or the key to the truth. Wake up.**
